So you're interested in Linux, you say?

Well, good! I guess the first question to answer is "What exactly is Linux, anyway?" The most common answer to that is that it's an operating system (more commonly referred to by OS) like Windows or Apple's macOS (we'll get into why that's only mostly correct here in a minute, but for now this definition will work.) It's used mostly on servers, but a growing number of people use it on their desktop PCs or laptops as well. Next question: "How's it different from Windows, or macOS?" Great one! To answer that best, we need a (brief) history lesson.

The first few decades of computers weren't like they are now; these days, there are a few major OSes, sure, but you can generally sit down at any computer and feel relatively comfortable using it. Back then each computer vendor had written their own OS. If you were using a computer, you had to know how to interact with that computer's OS. Using any given computer technology required being a specialist in that specific technology.

Then, in the mid-70s, a group of programmer types (I'm not going into too many details here, they're unimportant for our purposes, google it if you're curious) created Unix. This evolved over time, but by its fifth and final large revision, SysV, it had been ported to a pile of computer architectures and iterated upon, and for all intents and purposes had become the OS.

Unix's design philosophy is "Do one thing, and do it well." Its design is modular; instead of giant megaprograms designed to do lots of very specific tasks (think, say, Microsoft Word) it was made up of a pile of programs that only did one task, which could be assembled like Lego blocks on the fly for any larger scale task one might need.
